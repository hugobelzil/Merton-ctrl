\begin{abstract}
For continuous systems modeled by dynamical equations such as ODEs and SDEs, Bellman's principle of optimality takes the form of the Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman (HJB) equation, which provides the theoretical target of reinforcement learning (RL). Although recent advances in RL successfully leverage this formulation, the existing methods typically assume the underlying dynamics are known a priori because they need explicit access to the drift and diffusion coefficients to update the value function following the HJB equation. We address this inherent limitation of HJB-based RL; we propose a model-free approach still targeting the HJB equation and the corresponding temporal difference method. We prove exponential stability of the induced continuous-time dynamics, and we empirically demonstrate the resulting advantages over transition-kernel-based formulations. The proposed formulation paves the way toward bridging stochastic control and model-free reinforcement learning.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Reinforcement learning (RL) has been successfully applied in various domains, ranging from discrete systems like board games \cite{silver2018} to systems that are continuous in both state and time such as robotic control \cite{kober2013}. RL research has been advancing through a variety of approaches, and much of the work has focused on improving methods by refining objective functions \cite{schulman2015,schulman2017}, balancing exploration and exploitation \cite{haarnoja2018}, and developing more effective architectures \cite{hafner2019}. These efforts have significantly advanced the field, leading to the development of various successful algorithms.

In contrast to the studies primarily targeting algorithmic components or optimization techniques, we focus on the continuity of time and explore what we call continuous RL, where the dynamics of systems are described by ordinary differential equations (ODEs) or stochastic differential equations (SDEs), which is a relatively underexplored aspect of RL. Although many RL methods have been applied, sometimes heuristically, to both discrete and continuous systems, the continuity of the system has not necessarily been fully exploited, even when it is known in advance. Laying a foundation for utilizing prior knowledge of continuity is important toward more effective learning and decision-making methods, particularly for practical applications such as robot control and autonomous driving, which typically fall into the target of continuous RL.

A way to incorporate prior knowledge of the system's continuity into the learning process is to use the Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman (HJB) equation. In the Bellman equation, continuity is encoded in the transition kernel. However, in model-free RL, where transitions are approximated using samples, this continuity information is lost because the transition kernel is not explicitly modeled. On the other hand, the HJB equation retains the continuity information in the argument of the expectation rather than in the transition kernel. This property allows the HJB equation to keep taking continuity into account even under sample-based approximations. However, because the HJB equation depends on the coefficient functions of the system's dynamics, prior work has largely been limited to model-based approaches \cite{munos1997stochastic,yildiz2021}.

In this paper, we introduce a temporal difference (TD) method based on the HJB equation, namely differential TD (dTD), achieved through sample-based approximation of the expectation term in the HJB equation. dTD enables policy evaluation without requiring knowledge or estimation of the system dynamics, while incorporating the continuity of the dynamics into the learning process. It is compatible with on-policy methods such as A2C \cite{mnih2016} and PPO \cite{schulman2017}, and we demonstrate its effectiveness on Mujoco \cite{todorov2012} tasks including Hopper, HalfCheetah, Ant, and Humanoid. The code for the proposed method is available at \url{https://github.com/4thhia/differential_TD}.

\section{Related Work}
\paragraph{Deterministic dynamics.}
The study of continuous RL for ODE systems can be traced back to studies such as \citet{baird1994,munos1997,doya2000,munos2006}. \citet{baird1994} discovered that the Q-function collapses in continuous RL, which was rigorously proven and extended to deep RL in \citet{tallec2019}. In \citet{munos1997}, model-free approaches for ODE systems were first studied. \citet{doya2000} was the first to introduce TD for ODE systems and extended it to TD($\lambda$) and actor-critic. \citet{munos2006} investigated the estimation of policy gradients for ODE systems and proposed a pathwise derivative approach. More recently, \citet{vamvoudakis2017} developed a model-free RL framework for deterministic linear systems. \citet{kim2021} proposed a model-free Q-learning approach in which the control is derived from the HJB equation, while the learning target is based on the conventional Bellman equation. \citet{yildiz2021} introduced a model-based method that leverages the Neural ODE framework to enable continuous-time optimization using learned system dynamics.

\paragraph{Stochastic dynamics.}
One of the earliest works on RL in SDE systems is \citet{munos1997stochastic}, which takes a model-based approach. However, research in this direction remained largely unexplored until recently. A growing body of work has emerged that aims to establish theoretical foundations for RL in stochastic dynamics. \citet{wang2020} introduced an entropy-regularized relaxed control formulation and provided a comprehensive analysis in the LQR setting. \citet{tang2022} further demonstrated the well-posedness of the HJB equation within this relaxed control framework. \citet{jia2022b} showed that Bellman optimality is equivalent to maintaining the martingale property of a suitably defined stochastic process, and proposed a corresponding algorithm. Building on this approach, \citet{jia2022a,jia2023} proposed actor-critic and Q-learning algorithms for finite-horizon SDE systems, respectively. \citet{zhao2020} extended key theoretical tools such as the state visitation distribution and the performance difference lemma to the continuous-time setting and applied them to TRPO and PPO. In contrast to these approaches, our work proposes a simple model-free TD method that is applicable to general nonlinear stochastic dynamics without requiring knowledge of the SDE coefficients.

\section{Background}
\subsection{Problem Setting}
We consider a continuous-time RL setting where the state space is $\mathcal{S}\subset\R^n$ and the action space is $\mathcal{A}$. We model the state dynamics by the following controlled SDE:
\begin{equation}
\dd S_t=\mu(S_t,A_t)\dd t+\sigma(S_t,A_t)\dd B_t,
\label{eq:sde}
\end{equation}
where $\mu:\mathcal{S}\times\mathcal{A}\to\R^n$, $\sigma:\mathcal{S}\times\mathcal{A}\to\R^{n\times m}$, and $(B_t)_{t\ge0}$ is the $m$-dimensional Brownian motion. The state evolution is influenced by both the inherent noise in the system and the randomness induced by the stochastic policy $\pi:\mathcal{S}\to\mathcal{P}(\mathcal{A})$. Thus, expectations related to this SDE are expressed as $\E_{p^\pi}[\cdot]$, where $p^\pi$ denotes the transition kernel induced by \eqref{eq:sde}. We primarily focus on the model-free setting, where the agent has no prior knowledge of $\mu$ and $\sigma$.

\subsection{Continuous RL}
The dynamics governed by \eqref{eq:sde} exhibit the Markov property, and thus continuous RL falls within the general framework of an MDP. For example, the Bellman optimality equation can be written as
\begin{equation}
V^*(s_t)=\max_{\pi}\E_{p^\pi}\left[\int_t^{t'} e^{-\gamma(\tau-t)}\rho(S_\tau,A_\tau)\dd\tau
+e^{-\gamma(t'-t)}V^*(S_{t'})\mid S_t=s_t\right],
\end{equation}
where $\rho:\mathcal{S}\times\mathcal{A}\to\R$ is the reward rate function and $\gamma\in(0,\infty)$ is the discount rate. The optimal value function is
\begin{equation}
V^*(s):=\max_\pi\E_{p^\pi}\left[\int_t^\infty e^{-\gamma(\tau-t)}\rho(S_\tau,A_\tau)\dd\tau\mid S_t=s\right].
\end{equation}
When learning based on discrete observations of a continuous system, one can discretize the problem as
\begin{equation}
V^*(s_t)=\max_\pi\E_{p^\pi}\left[\rho(s_t,A_t)\dt+e^{-\gamma\dt}V^*(S_{t+\dt})\right].
\label{eq:bellman-opt}
\end{equation}
Standard RL methods can be applied to this discretization, although the use of Q-functions in continuous time requires care \cite{tallec2019}. In the model-free setting, however, the dynamics enter only through the transition kernel, and approximating the expectation by samples does not explicitly exploit the underlying continuity of the dynamics.

\subsection{Natural Target of Continuous RL}
We focus on TD learning. Combining Bellman optimality or expectation equations with $V$ or $Q$ functions gives four candidate targets. The $V$-Bellman optimality equation is immediately excluded as it is not compatible with the model-free framework. The $Q$-Bellman optimality equation is challenging in continuous RL due to the maximization operator. Since Q-functions often degenerate in continuous RL \cite{tallec2019}, we focus on policy evaluation via the $V$-Bellman expectation equation,
\begin{equation}
V^\pi(s_t)=\E_{p^\pi}\left[\rho(s_t,A_t)\dt+e^{-\gamma\dt}V^\pi(S_{t+\dt})\right].
\label{eq:bellman-exp}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Contrast with Stochastic Control}
Prior work on continuous RL often starts by introducing the HJB equation, the continuous counterpart of \eqref{eq:bellman-opt}. For instance:
\begin{equation}
V^*(s_t)=\frac{1}{\gamma}\max_\pi \E_\pi\left[
\rho(s_t,A_t)+\sum_{i=1}^{n}\mu^i(s_t,A_t)\frac{\partial V^*(s)}{\partial s_i}\bigg|_{s=s_t}
+\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{j=1}^{n}
[\sigma(s_t,A_t)\sigma^\top(s_t,A_t)]^{ij}
\frac{\partial^2 V^*(s)}{\partial s_i\partial s_j}\bigg|_{s=s_t}
\right].
\label{eq:hjb-opt}
\end{equation}
However, introducing the HJB equation typically restricts the methodology to model-based approaches, since the update requires explicit access to $\mu$ and $\sigma$. Our goal is to design model-free algorithms that explicitly leverage continuity, which leads us to a fixed-policy variant of the HJB equation.

\section{TD Method for Stochastic Continuous Dynamics}
Embedding the SDE information in the argument of the expectation rather than only in the transition kernel can keep continuity information from being lost under sample approximation. Expanding $V(S_{t+\dt})$ via It\^o's formula gives the fixed-policy HJB equation
\begin{equation}
V^\pi(s_t)=\frac{1}{\gamma}\E_\pi\left[
\rho(s_t,A_t)+\sum_{i=1}^{n}\mu^i(s_t,A_t)\frac{\partial V^\pi(s)}{\partial s_i}\bigg|_{s_t}
+\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{j=1}^{n}
[\sigma(s_t,A_t)\sigma^\top(s_t,A_t)]^{ij}
\frac{\partial^2 V^\pi(s)}{\partial s_i\partial s_j}\bigg|_{s_t}
\right].
\label{eq:fixed-policy-hjb}
\end{equation}
This still contains $\mu$ and $\sigma$, so it is not directly model-free.

\subsection{Deriving TD from the HJB Equation}
\begin{proposition}
When $(S_t)_{t\ge0}$ follows the SDE in \eqref{eq:sde}, we have
\begin{equation}
\E_{p^\pi}[\mu^i(s_t,A_t)]
=\lim_{\dt\to0}\E_{p^\pi}\left[\frac{S^i_{t+\dt}-s^i_t}{\dt}\right],
\label{eq:drift-limit}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
\E_{p^\pi}\left[[\sigma(s_t,A_t)\sigma^\top(s_t,A_t)]^{ij}\right]
=\lim_{\dt\to0}\E_{p^\pi}\left[
\frac{(S^i_{t+\dt}-s^i_t)(S^j_{t+\dt}-s^j_t)}{\dt}
\right].
\label{eq:diffusion-limit}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For the first claim, expand the $i$-th component of \eqref{eq:sde}:
\begin{equation}
S^i_{t+\dt}=s^i_t+\mu^i(s_t,A_t)\dt+
\sum_{j=1}^{m}\sigma^{ij}(s_t,A_t)(B^j_{t+\dt}-B^j_t)+O(\dt^{3/2}).
\end{equation}
Since $B^j_{t+\dt}-B^j_t$ is zero-mean Gaussian and independent of the state and action, taking expectations and letting $\dt\to0$ yields \eqref{eq:drift-limit}. For the second claim, consider
\begin{align}
(S^i_{t+\dt}-s^i_t)(S^j_{t+\dt}-s^j_t)
&=\mu^i(s_t,A_t)\mu^j(s_t,A_t)\dt^2 \notag\\
&\quad+\sum_{k=1}^{m}\sum_{\ell=1}^{m}\sigma^{ik}(s_t,A_t)\sigma^{j\ell}(s_t,A_t)
(B^k_{t+\dt}-B^k_t)(B^\ell_{t+\dt}-B^\ell_t) \notag\\
&\quad+\mu^i(s_t,A_t)\dt\sum_{k=1}^{m}\sigma^{jk}(s_t,A_t)(B^k_{t+\dt}-B^k_t) \notag\\
&\quad+\mu^j(s_t,A_t)\dt\sum_{k=1}^{m}\sigma^{ik}(s_t,A_t)(B^k_{t+\dt}-B^k_t)+O(\dt^{3/2}).
\end{align}
Using $\E[(B^k_{t+\dt}-B^k_t)(B^\ell_{t+\dt}-B^\ell_t)]=\delta_{k\ell}\dt$ and letting $\dt\to0$ gives \eqref{eq:diffusion-limit}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Since $S_{t+\dt}$ is sampled under policy $\pi$, the method is not applicable to off-policy settings such as value iteration or Q-learning. The formulation relies on the HJB equation under a fixed policy.
\end{remark}

From Proposition 1, the fixed-policy HJB equation can be reformulated without explicit access to $\mu$ and $\sigma$:
\begin{equation}
V^\pi(s_t)=\frac{1}{\gamma}\lim_{\dt\to0}\E_{p^\pi}\left[
\rho(s_t,A_t)+\sum_{i=1}^{n}\frac{S^i_{t+\dt}-s^i_t}{\dt}\frac{\partial V^\pi(s)}{\partial s_i}\bigg|_{s_t}
+\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{j=1}^{n}
\frac{(S^i_{t+\dt}-s^i_t)(S^j_{t+\dt}-s^j_t)}{\dt}
\frac{\partial^2 V^\pi(s)}{\partial s_i\partial s_j}\bigg|_{s_t}
\right].
\end{equation}

\begin{definition}[Differential temporal difference]
Let $\dt>0$ be a time step and $\widehat V$ denote an estimated value function. The dTD is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{dTD}:=\frac{1}{\gamma}\left(
\rho(s_t,a_t)+\sum_{i=1}^{n}\frac{s^i_{t+\dt}-s^i_t}{\dt}
\frac{\partial\widehat V(s)}{\partial s_i}\bigg|_{s_t}
+\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{j=1}^{n}
\frac{(s^i_{t+\dt}-s^i_t)(s^j_{t+\dt}-s^j_t)}{\dt}
\frac{\partial^2\widehat V(s)}{\partial s_i\partial s_j}\bigg|_{s_t}
\right)-\widehat V(s_t).
\label{eq:dtd}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\subsection{Convergence Analysis}
A standard TD convergence analysis relies on the Bellman operator being a contraction. In contrast, the fixed-policy HJB operator involves unbounded differential operators and is not generally a contraction. We adopt an idealized function-space analysis and study the continuous-time limit of the induced dynamics.

\begin{definition}[Fixed-policy HJB operator]
Given a stationary Markov policy $\pi$, discount rate $\gamma>0$, and functions $\mu,\sigma,\rho$, define
\[
\bar\mu(s):=\E_{A\sim\pi(\cdot|s)}[\mu(s,A)],\quad
D(s):=\E_{A\sim\pi(\cdot|s)}[\sigma(s,A)\sigma^\top(s,A)],\quad
\bar\rho(s):=\E_{A\sim\pi(\cdot|s)}[\rho(s,A)].
\]
The fixed-policy HJB operator $T$ maps $V:\mathcal{S}\to\R$ to
\[
(TV)(s):=\frac{1}{\gamma}\left(\bar\rho(s)+(L^\pi V)(s)\right).
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Infinitesimal generator]
The infinitesimal generator $L^\pi$ maps any $C^2$ function $V$ to
\[
(L^\pi V)(s):=\bar\mu(s)\cdot\nabla V(s)+\frac{1}{2}\mathrm{tr}\left(D(s)\nabla^2V(s)\right).
\]
\end{definition}

We analyze the iterative scheme $V_{k+1}=V_k+\eta_k(TV_k-V_k)$ through its continuous-time limit
\[
\frac{\partial V(t)}{\partial t}=TV(t)-V(t).
\]
The fixed-point equation $TV=V$ is equivalent to
\[
(\gamma I-L^\pi)V(s)=\bar\rho(s).
\]

\begin{assumption}
We assume: (i) $\mathcal{S}\subset\R^n$ is bounded with $C^2$ boundary $\partial\mathcal{S}$; (ii) $\bar\rho\in L^2(\mathcal{S})$, $D\in W^{1,\infty}(\mathcal{S};\R^{n\times n})$ is symmetric, and $b:=\bar\mu-\frac{1}{2}\mathrm{div}\,D\in L^\infty(\mathcal{S};\R^n)$; (iii) there exists $\alpha>0$ such that $\xi^\top D(s)\xi\ge\alpha\|\xi\|^2$; (iv) the Neumann condition $n\cdot D\nabla V=0$ holds on $\partial\mathcal{S}$; and (v) $\gamma>\|b\|_\infty^2/\alpha$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{lemma}[Existence and uniqueness of the fixed point]
Under Assumption 1, the linear elliptic PDE
\[
(\gamma I-L^\pi)V(s)=\bar\rho(s),\qquad s\in\mathcal{S},
\]
admits a unique weak solution $V^\pi\in H^1(\mathcal{S})$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proposition}[Exponential stability of the dynamics]
Let $V^\pi\in H^1(\mathcal{S})$ be the unique weak solution of $(\gamma I-L^\pi)V=\bar\rho$. Under Assumption 1, the solution $V(t)$ of
\[
\frac{\partial V(t)}{\partial t}=TV(t)-V(t)
\]
satisfies, for any $V(0)\in H^1(\mathcal{S})$,
\[
\|V(t)-V^\pi\|_{L^2(\mathcal{S})}\le
\exp(-\lambda t)\|V(0)-V^\pi\|_{L^2(\mathcal{S})},
\]
where
\[
\lambda=\frac{1}{\gamma}\left(\gamma-\frac{\|b\|_\infty^2}{\alpha}\right)>0,\qquad
b:=\bar\mu-\frac{1}{2}\mathrm{div}\,D.
\]
\end{proposition}

\section{Method}
This section outlines how dTD is applied in deep RL. We represent the value function with a neural network; pseudocode is provided in Appendix~\ref{app:algorithm}.

\subsection{Loss Function}
In deep RL, TD methods typically use a fixed target, $r(s,t)+\gamma_{\mathrm{discrete}}V(s_{t+1})$, as the teacher and minimize squared error to the prediction $V(s_t)$. Although it may seem natural to treat $V(s_t)$ in \eqref{eq:dtd} as the prediction and the remaining terms as the target, the split between prediction and target is a design choice. We examine two variants:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{naive-dTD}: treats $V(s_t)$ as the prediction, following the classical TD-style decomposition.
\item \textbf{dTD}: treats value-based terms as the target and derivative-based terms as the prediction.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Hybrid Scheme for Stabilizing dTD}
Because Proposition 2 relies on an idealized setting and does not cover practical instability from function approximation, we linearly combine the classical TD error with the dTD error, using weights $1-\beta$ and $\beta$, respectively. We call the resulting update $\beta$-dTD. The TD part supplies empirical stability, whereas the dTD part injects gradient information from continuous dynamics.

\section{Experiments}
\subsection{Modification for Discrete Environment Compatibility}
The continuous reward formulation is approximated by
\[
\int_0^\infty e^{-\gamma t}\rho(s_t,a_t)\dd t\approx
\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}e^{(-\gamma\Delta t)k}\rho(s_{k\Delta t},a_{k\Delta t})\Delta t.
\]
Thus $\rho(s_t,a_t)\Delta t$ corresponds to the discrete reward $r$, and $e^{-\gamma\Delta t}$ corresponds to the discrete discount ratio $\gamma_{\mathrm{discrete}}$:
\[
\rho(s_t,a_t)=\frac{r(s_t,a_t)}{\Delta t},\qquad
\gamma=-\frac{1}{\Delta t}\log(\gamma_{\mathrm{discrete}}).
\]
With this scaling, dTD can be computed as
\[
\text{dTD Target}: -r(s_t,a_t)-\log(\gamma_{\mathrm{discrete}})V(s_{t+\Delta t}),
\]
\[
\text{dTD Prediction}:
\sum_{i=1}^{n}(s^i_{t+\Delta t}-s^i_t)\frac{\partial V(s)}{\partial s_i}\bigg|_{s_t}
+\frac12\sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{j=1}^{n}(s^i_{t+\Delta t}-s^i_t)(s^j_{t+\Delta t}-s^j_t)
\frac{\partial^2 V(s)}{\partial s_i\partial s_j}\bigg|_{s_t}.
\]

\subsection{Experiment Design}
\paragraph{Environment.}
We conducted experiments with the Brax library \cite{freeman2021} in Hopper, HalfCheetah, Ant, and Humanoid. Each environment provides a mid- to high-dimensional state space: Hopper has 11 dimensions, HalfCheetah 17, Ant 27, and Humanoid 244. At every step, each state component was perturbed by
\[
s^i\leftarrow s^i+\mathrm{coef}\times|s^i|\times\mathrm{noise},
\qquad \mathrm{noise}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1),
\]
with $\mathrm{coef}\in\{0.00,0.01,0.05\}$. The time steps were Hopper: $\Delta t=0.008$, HalfCheetah: $\Delta t=0.05$, Ant: $\Delta t=0.05$, and Humanoid: $\Delta t=0.015$.

\paragraph{Baseline.}
We used standard TD methods as baselines and experimented with TD, $\beta$-naive-dTD, and $\beta$-dTD, using A2C \cite{mnih2016} and PPO \cite{schulman2017}. PPO results are the primary focus; A2C results are in Appendix~\ref{app:a2c}.

\paragraph{Hyperparameter tuning.}
For hyperparameter tuning, we applied DEHB \cite{awad2021}, a multi-fidelity method that is effective in RL \cite{eimer2023}. Additional search spaces are in Appendix~\ref{app:hparams}.

\subsection{Results and Discussion}
\paragraph{Comparative evaluation.}
Figure~\ref{fig:ppo} compares the proposed method and the baseline. $\beta$-dTD consistently outperforms $\beta$-naive-dTD. In all environments, optimized $\beta$ values for $\beta$-naive-dTD were small, suggesting that its effective update rule became close to standard TD; nevertheless, it remained significantly worse than standard TD. By contrast, $\beta$-dTD outperforms TD or achieves comparable performance in all cases. Although final performance can converge because TD and dTD are derived from the same Bellman equation, dTD can make more informative updates by using continuity information during training.

\paragraph{Significance of dTD.}
In contrast to $\beta$-naive-dTD, the weight $\beta$ in $\beta$-dTD is not exceedingly small. In HalfCheetah, $\beta$ is 0.74, indicating that dTD retains a meaningful impact on learning.

\paragraph{Impact of process noise.}
Both $\beta$-dTD and TD exhibit similar robustness to process noise. With $\mathrm{coef}=0.01$, neither method degrades substantially. With $\mathrm{coef}=0.05$, both show reduced performance, particularly in Ant and HalfCheetah.

\section{Conclusion}
We presented differential TD (dTD), a temporal difference method based on the HJB equation. In contrast to approaches based on transition kernels, the proposed method can incorporate the continuity of dynamics into the learning process without knowing the dynamics. We showed empirical results for continuous control environments with different time intervals. The results highlight the potential advantages of dTD in learning speed and efficiency while also implying that stability concerns may exist in practice, motivating the robust $\beta$-dTD update.

We also analyzed conditions under which the continuous-time dynamics of the HJB equation exhibit exponential stability toward the unique fixed point. This stability property, proven using linear elliptic PDE theory, is crucial for showing theoretical convergence of the idealized iterative scheme. However, sufficient conditions such as bounded domains and uniform ellipticity are often hard to maintain or verify in deep RL with function approximation.

Future work includes bridging the gap between theoretical exponential stability and practical stability of dTD updates, reducing variance through improved estimators or regularization, and extending TD-based techniques to the dTD framework.

\section{Mathematical Details}
\subsection{Justification for the Continuous RL Formulation}
In Section 3, we modeled the state under a stochastic policy by
\[
\dd S_t=\mu(S_t,A_t)\dd t+\sigma(S_t,A_t)\dd B_t,\qquad A_t\sim\pi(\cdot|S_t).
\]
The presence of external randomness from stochastic action selection introduces analytical difficulties. Prior work often adopts averaged dynamics $(\widetilde S_t)_{t\ge0}$, whose marginal distribution at each time coincides with the original one under the same initial condition:
\[
\dd\widetilde S_t=\widetilde\mu(\widetilde S_t,\pi)\dd t+\widetilde\sigma(\widetilde S_t,\pi)\dd\widetilde B_t,
\]
where
\[
\widetilde\mu(s,\pi)=\int_\mathcal{A}\mu(s,a)\pi(a)\dd a,\qquad
\widetilde\sigma(s,\pi)=\left(\int_\mathcal{A}\sigma(s,a)\sigma^\top(s,a)\pi(a)\dd a\right)^{1/2}.
\]
Since the marginal distributions coincide, the value functions also coincide:
\[
V^\pi(s)=\E_{p^\pi}\left[\int_t^\infty e^{-\gamma(\tau-t)}\rho(S_\tau,A_\tau)\dd\tau\mid S_t=s\right]
=\E_{\widetilde p}\left[\int_t^\infty e^{-\gamma(\tau-t)}\widetilde\rho(\widetilde S_\tau,\pi)\dd\tau\mid \widetilde S_t=s\right],
\]
where $\widetilde\rho(s,\pi)=\int_\mathcal{A}\rho(s,a)\pi(a)\dd a$.

\subsection{It\^o Formula}
Assuming $(S_t)_{t\ge0}$ follows \eqref{eq:sde}, the term $V^*(S_{t+\Delta t})$ can be expanded using It\^o's lemma:
\begin{align}
V^*(s_t)
&=\max_\pi\E_{p^\pi}\left[\rho(s_t,A_t)\Delta t+e^{-\gamma\Delta t}V^*(S_{t+\Delta t})\right]\notag\\
&=\max_\pi\E_{p^\pi}\Bigg[
\rho(s_t,A_t)\Delta t+e^{-\gamma\Delta t}\Bigg\{V^*(s_t)
+\left(\sum_{i=1}^{n}\mu^i(s_t,A_t)\partial_iV^*(s_t)\right.\notag\\
&\qquad\left.
+\frac12\sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{j=1}^{n}[\sigma(s_t,A_t)\sigma^\top(s_t,A_t)]^{ij}\partial_{ij}V^*(s_t)\right)\Delta t
+O(\Delta t^{3/2})\Bigg\}\Bigg].
\end{align}
Simplifying and taking the limit as $\Delta t\to0$ gives \eqref{eq:hjb-opt}.

\subsection{Convergence of dTD}
\subsubsection{Proof of Lemma 1}
Consider
\[
(\gamma I-L^\pi)V=\bar\rho\quad\text{in }\mathcal{S},\qquad n\cdot D\nabla V=0\quad\text{on }\partial\mathcal{S},
\]
where
\[
(L^\pi V)(s)=\frac12\nabla\cdot(D(s)\nabla V(s))+b(s)\cdot\nabla V(s),\qquad
b:=\bar\mu-\frac12\mathrm{div}\,D.
\]
For $v\in H^1(\mathcal{S})$, integration by parts gives the weak formulation
\[
B(V,v)=f(v),
\]
where
\[
B(u,v):=\int_\mathcal{S}\gamma uv+\frac12\int_\mathcal{S}(D\nabla u)\cdot\nabla v-\int_\mathcal{S}(b\cdot\nabla u)v,\qquad
f(v):=\int_\mathcal{S}\bar\rho v.
\]
Since $D,b$ are bounded, $B$ is bounded on $H^1(\mathcal{S})$. Since $\bar\rho\in L^2(\mathcal{S})$, $f$ is bounded. Uniform ellipticity and Young's inequality give
\[
B(v,v)\ge\left(\gamma-\frac{\|b\|_\infty^2}{\alpha}\right)\|v\|_{L^2}^2+\frac{\alpha}{4}\|\nabla v\|_{L^2}^2.
\]
Under Assumption 1, $B$ is coercive. The Lax-Milgram theorem implies a unique weak solution $V^\pi\in H^1(\mathcal{S})$.

\subsubsection{Proof of Proposition 2}
Let $e(t):=V(t)-V^\pi$. Since $V^\pi$ satisfies $(\gamma I-L^\pi)V^\pi=\bar\rho$ and
\[
\partial_tV=-\gamma^{-1}(\gamma I-L^\pi)V+\gamma^{-1}\bar\rho,
\]
we obtain
\[
\partial_t e(t)=-\frac1\gamma(\gamma I-L^\pi)e(t).
\]
Using the weak formulation with test function $e(t)$,
\[
\frac12\frac{\dd}{\dd t}\|e(t)\|_{L^2(\mathcal{S})}^2
=-\frac1\gamma B(e(t),e(t)).
\]
The coercivity estimate gives
\[
\frac{\dd}{\dd t}\|e(t)\|_{L^2(\mathcal{S})}^2
\le-\frac2\gamma\left(\gamma-\frac{\|b\|_\infty^2}{\alpha}\right)
\|e(t)\|_{L^2(\mathcal{S})}^2.
\]
Gr\"onwall's inequality proves the claimed exponential bound.

\section{Implementation Details}
\subsection{Algorithm}
\label{app:algorithm}
\begin{enumerate}
\item Initialize value function $V_\theta$ with random parameter $\theta$.
\item For each training step, collect transitions $(s_t,a_t,s_{t+\Delta t},\rho_t)$ under policy $\pi$ into buffer $\mathcal{D}$.
\item For each update step, sample a batch from $\mathcal{D}$, freeze a copy $\bar\theta\leftarrow\theta$, compute dTD targets and predictions, and update $\theta$ by minimizing the squared dTD residual.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Efficient Computation of the dTD Loss}
Although \eqref{eq:dtd} involves the Hessian, one can avoid directly calculating all $O(n^2)$ entries by rearranging
\[
\left\langle\Delta s_t,\frac{\partial^2V(s)}{\partial s^2}\bigg|_{s_t}\Delta s_t\right\rangle
=
\left\langle\Delta s_t,\frac{\partial}{\partial s}
\left\langle\frac{\partial V}{\partial s},\Delta s_t\right\rangle\bigg|_{s_t}\right\rangle,
\]
which reduces practical computation to Hessian-vector products.

\subsection{Computing Infrastructure and Reproducibility}
Experiments were conducted on a machine with four NVIDIA Tesla V100 GPUs (32GB each) and an Intel Xeon E5-2698 v4 CPU. Hyperparameter tuning typically took 6--9 hours depending on the environment. Training time for final runs ranged from 10 to 60 minutes. All experiments used fixed random seeds in the training scripts, though full determinism cannot be ensured because MuJoCo accessed via Brax uses an internal random seed. The code is available at \url{https://github.com/4thhia/differential_TD}.
